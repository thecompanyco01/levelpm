How to Value a Property Management Company (2026 Guide)

Published March 8, 2026 · 12 min read

Whether you're buying, selling, or just curious what your PM company is worth, valuation is one of the most important (and misunderstood) topics in property management. Most owners either wildly overestimate their company's value or sell for far less than they should.

This guide breaks down every valuation method used in real PM acquisitions — with actual numbers, not theory.

The 3 Main Valuation Methods

1. Revenue Multiple Method

The most common approach. You take your annual recurring management fee revenue and multiply it by a factor, typically between 1.0x and 3.0x.

Company ProfileRevenue MultipleExample (at $500K revenue)
Small, owner-dependent, no systems1.0x – 1.5x$500K – $750K
Mid-size, some SOPs, mixed contracts1.5x – 2.0x$750K – $1M
Systemized, strong contracts, diverse portfolio2.0x – 2.5x$1M – $1.25M
Premium: brand, team, technology, growth2.5x – 3.0x+$1.25M – $1.5M+

Key insight: Only management fee revenue counts. Maintenance markups, leasing fees, and one-time income are usually excluded or heavily discounted because they're not predictable.

2. Per-Door Valuation

A quick-and-dirty method that values the company based on the number of doors (units) under management.

Market TypePrice Per DoorExample (200 doors)
Rural / low-rent markets$200 – $400$40K – $80K
Suburban / mid-market$400 – $800$80K – $160K
Urban / high-rent markets$800 – $1,500$160K – $300K
Premium metros (SF, NYC, LA)$1,500 – $2,500+$300K – $500K+

Per-door valuations are useful for quick comparisons but miss crucial factors like profitability, contract quality, and owner dependency.

3. EBITDA Multiple Method

The most sophisticated approach. EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization) reflects actual profitability.

Typical PM company EBITDA multiples range from 3x to 7x, depending on size, growth rate, and transferability.

EBITDA RangeMultipleWho's Buying
<$100K3x – 4xIndividual buyers, small competitors
$100K – $500K4x – 5xRegional consolidators
$500K – $1M5x – 6xPE-backed platforms
$1M+6x – 8x+Large PE firms, public companies

What Drives Value Up (Premium Factors)

  1. Documented SOPs and systems — If the business runs without the owner, it's worth significantly more. Buyers pay a premium for transferability.
  2. Long-term management agreements — 12+ month contracts with auto-renewal are gold. Month-to-month agreements reduce value by 20-40%.
  3. Diversified portfolio — No single owner should represent more than 10% of revenue. Concentration risk kills valuation.
  4. Low owner dependency — If you're doing property inspections yourself, that's a red flag for buyers.
  5. Clean financials — Separate trust accounts, clean P&L, tax returns that match stated revenue.
  6. Growth trajectory — Companies adding 3-5+ doors per month command premium multiples.
  7. Technology stack — Modern PM software (AppFolio, Buildium, Rent Manager) signals operational maturity.
  8. Strong online reputation — 4.5+ stars on Google with 50+ reviews matters more than you think.

What Kills Value (Discount Factors)

  • Owner IS the business — If clients stay because of a personal relationship with the owner, expect 30-50% haircut
  • Deferred maintenance — Portfolios with a backlog of unresolved maintenance issues scare buyers
  • High churn rate — Losing more than 15% of doors per year signals underlying problems
  • Pending lawsuits or compliance issues — Unresolved legal matters can tank a deal entirely
  • Commingled trust accounts — This is an automatic disqualifier for serious buyers
  • No written procedures — Buyers see chaos they'll need to fix, and they price that in

The Acquisition Process: What to Expect

For Sellers

  1. Preparation (3-6 months before) — Clean up financials, document all SOPs, lock in management agreements, reduce owner dependency
  2. Valuation — Get 2-3 independent valuations. Use all three methods above and triangulate
  3. Finding buyers — Reach out to regional competitors, PM consolidators (like Evernest, Real Property Management), and business brokers who specialize in PM
  4. Due diligence — Expect buyers to review contracts, financials, trust accounts, maintenance logs, online reviews, and employee agreements
  5. Deal structure — Most PM acquisitions include an earn-out: 50-70% at close, remainder over 12-24 months based on door retention

For Buyers

  1. Define acquisition criteria — Market, size, property type, price range
  2. Source deals — NARPM conferences, business brokers, direct outreach to PM owners
  3. Due diligence checklist:
    • Last 3 years of tax returns and P&L statements
    • All management agreements (read every one)
    • Trust account reconciliation reports
    • Door count trend (monthly for last 24 months)
    • Online reviews and reputation audit
    • Employee contracts and non-compete agreements
    • Technology and software licenses
    • Pending maintenance requests
  4. Retention risk assessment — Call a sample of owners to gauge satisfaction and likelihood of staying post-acquisition

Real-World Valuation Examples

Example 1: Small PM Company

Profile: 80 doors, $120K management fee revenue, owner-operated, month-to-month contracts, basic PM software

Valuation: 1.0x – 1.2x revenue = $120K – $144K. Per-door: $400 × 80 = $32K (indicates revenue method is more appropriate). Realistic value: ~$130K

Example 2: Mid-Size Systemized Company

Profile: 350 doors, $480K management fee revenue, 2 PMs + admin, SOPs documented, 12-month contracts, AppFolio, 4.7 stars

Valuation: 2.0x – 2.3x revenue = $960K – $1.1M. EBITDA of ~$150K × 5 = $750K. Realistic value: ~$900K – $1M

Example 3: Premium Growth Company

Profile: 800 doors, $1.2M management fee revenue, full team, strong SOPs, 24-month contracts, 4.9 stars, adding 10 doors/month

Valuation: 2.5x – 3.0x revenue = $3M – $3.6M. EBITDA of $400K × 7 = $2.8M. Realistic value: ~$3M+

How to Increase Your Company's Value Starting Today

  1. Document everything — Start with your top 5 workflows. Maintenance triage, move-in/out, owner onboarding, lease renewal, tenant screening. Written SOPs make your company transferable.
  2. Lock in contracts — Move all owners from month-to-month to 12+ month management agreements with 60-90 day cancellation clauses.
  3. Reduce owner dependency — Stop being the single point of contact. Train your team to handle owner calls and property inspections.
  4. Clean your financials — Separate business and personal expenses completely. Use proper PM accounting with trust account reconciliation.
  5. Grow systematically — Add doors predictably. 3-5 per month shows a growth engine, not just opportunistic additions.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much is a 100-door property management company worth?

Typically $100K – $300K depending on revenue per door, contract quality, and systems. A systemized 100-door company with strong contracts can be worth 2-3x a disorganized one with the same door count.

What's the most important factor in PM company valuation?

Transferability. Can the business operate and retain clients without the current owner? Everything else — revenue, doors, reputation — is secondary to this question.

How long does it take to sell a PM company?

Typically 6-12 months from decision to close. Well-prepared companies with clean financials and documented systems sell faster and at higher multiples.

Should I use a broker to sell my PM company?

For companies under 200 doors, you can likely sell directly to a local competitor or regional consolidator. For 200+ doors, a broker who specializes in PM transactions is worth the 8-12% commission because they'll attract more buyers and negotiate higher multiples.
