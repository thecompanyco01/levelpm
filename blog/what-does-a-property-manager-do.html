What Does a Property Manager Do? Complete Role Breakdown

Updated March 2026 · 11 min read · By ScaleDoors Team

If you're wondering what does a property manager do — whether you're considering hiring one, becoming one, or scaling your own PM company — this guide covers every responsibility, task, and skill involved. From daily operations to strategic portfolio management.

Property Manager: The 30-Second Answer

A property manager handles the day-to-day operations of rental properties on behalf of owners. This includes finding tenants, collecting rent, coordinating maintenance, handling finances, and ensuring legal compliance. They earn 8-12% of monthly rent (residential) or 4-8% (commercial) for this service.

Core Responsibilities

1. Tenant Acquisition & Screening

Finding good tenants is arguably the most important PM function. Bad tenants cost owners $5,000-30,000+ in evictions, damage, and lost rent.

  • Marketing vacant units — listing on Zillow, Apartments.com, MLS, social media, and the PM company website
  • Showing properties — scheduling and conducting tours (increasingly via self-showing technology)
  • Screening applicants — credit checks, criminal background, employment verification, rental history, income verification (3x rent minimum is standard)
  • Executing leases — drafting, reviewing, and signing lease agreements that comply with state/local law

2. Rent Collection & Financial Management

  • Collecting rent — setting up online payment portals, processing checks, enforcing late fees
  • Paying expenses — mortgage, insurance, HOA, property taxes, vendor invoices
  • Owner distributions — sending net income to owners (typically monthly)
  • Financial reporting — monthly statements, annual reports, tax documents (1099s)
  • Trust accounting — managing security deposits and owner funds in compliance with state regulations
  • Budgeting — creating annual operating budgets and capital expenditure plans

3. Maintenance & Repairs

Maintenance is the #1 source of tenant satisfaction (or frustration) and owner expense concerns.

  • Handling work orders — receiving, categorizing, and dispatching maintenance requests
  • Vendor management — maintaining relationships with plumbers, electricians, HVAC techs, handymen, and contractors
  • Preventive maintenance — scheduling seasonal inspections, HVAC servicing, gutter cleaning, etc.
  • Emergency response — 24/7 emergency line for water leaks, lockouts, fire, HVAC failures
  • Capital improvements — recommending and overseeing renovations, appliance replacements, and upgrades

4. Legal Compliance

  • Fair housing compliance — ensuring all marketing, screening, and tenant interactions follow federal, state, and local fair housing laws
  • Eviction management — serving notices, filing court documents, coordinating with attorneys, managing the process
  • Lease enforcement — addressing violations (pets, noise, unauthorized occupants, property damage)
  • Local ordinance compliance — rental registrations, inspections, lead paint disclosures, habitability standards
  • Safety compliance — smoke detectors, carbon monoxide detectors, fire extinguishers, emergency exits

5. Owner Relations

  • Regular communication — updates on vacancies, maintenance, market conditions, and financial performance
  • Rent analysis — recommending rent amounts based on market comparables
  • Investment advice — identifying value-add opportunities, recommending improvements that increase ROI
  • Tax support — providing year-end income/expense statements and 1099 forms

Types of Property Management

  • Residential single-family (SFR) — Managing individual rental homes. Most common for small PM companies. Fee: 8-12% of rent.
  • Multifamily — Apartment buildings and complexes. Usually has on-site staff. Fee: 4-8% of gross rent.
  • Commercial — Office, retail, industrial. More complex leases, CAM charges, tenant buildouts. Fee: 4-6% of gross rent.
  • HOA/Condo — Community management, common area maintenance, board communication. Fee: $10-20/unit/month.
  • Short-term rental (STR/Airbnb) — High-touch, revenue optimization, guest communication. Fee: 15-25% of revenue.

FAQ

Q: How much does a property manager cost?
Residential: 8-12% of monthly rent + a leasing fee (50-100% of first month's rent) for placing tenants. Some charge flat fees ($100-300/unit/month).

Q: Is property management a good career?
Yes — strong demand, above-average pay ($50K-75K for experienced managers), clear advancement path (manager → regional → VP), and the option to start your own company.

Q: Can a property manager be held personally liable?
Generally no, if they operate through an LLC with proper insurance. But negligence, fair housing violations, or trust accounting fraud can create personal liability.

Q: What's the difference between a property manager and a landlord?
A landlord owns the property. A property manager operates it on behalf of the owner. Some landlords self-manage; others hire a PM company. See our landlord vs. PM comparison.

Bottom Line

Property managers are operational CEOs for rental portfolios. They handle everything from marketing vacancies to eviction proceedings, from midnight plumbing emergencies to annual financial reporting. It's a demanding role that requires a wide skill set — but for those who build systems and scale, it's also a lucrative business.
