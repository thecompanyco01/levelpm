Property Management Maintenance: The Complete Operations Guide

Triage systems, vendor management, preventive maintenance schedules, emergency protocols, and cost control strategies.

Home → Blog → PM Maintenance Guide

Maintenance is the #1 operational challenge in property management. It's also the #1 driver of tenant satisfaction and the #1 source of owner complaints about costs. Get maintenance right and everything else gets easier. Get it wrong and your business drowns in chaos.

The Maintenance Triage System

Every maintenance request must be categorized immediately upon receipt. No exceptions. Use this four-tier system:

PriorityResponse TimeExamplesOwner Approval
🔴 EmergencyWithin 1 hourGas leak, flooding, fire damage, no heat in winter, sewage backup, break-inNo — act immediately, notify after
🟠 UrgentWithin 24 hoursNo hot water, A/C failure in summer, refrigerator not working, major plumbing leakUnder $500: No. Over $500: Yes
🔵 Routine2-5 business daysRunning toilet, dripping faucet, minor electrical, appliance issues, pest controlUnder $300: No. Over $300: Yes
🟢 ScheduledNext available windowCosmetic repairs, preventive maintenance, upgrades, non-urgent improvementsAlways get approval

Vendor Management

Your vendors make or break your maintenance operation. Here's how to build and manage a reliable vendor network:

The Core Vendor Roster

Every PM company needs reliable vendors in these categories:

  • General handyman — handles 60% of routine requests (minimum 2 reliable handymen)
  • Plumber — both routine and emergency (need 24/7 availability)
  • Electrician — licensed, insured, responsive
  • HVAC technician — critical for tenant comfort; need emergency availability
  • Locksmith — lockouts, rekeys, security upgrades
  • Cleaning crew — unit turnovers (speed matters)
  • Painter — turnovers and touch-ups
  • Landscaper — exterior maintenance (if applicable)
  • Pest control — preventive and reactive
  • Roofer — less frequent but critical when needed

Vendor Qualification Checklist

Before adding any vendor to your roster:

  1. ✅ Verify license (state contractor's license where required)
  2. ✅ Verify insurance (general liability $1M minimum, workers' comp)
  3. ✅ Get insurance certificate naming your company as additional insured
  4. ✅ Check references (3 minimum from other PM companies)
  5. ✅ Agree on pricing (hourly rate or flat rates for common tasks)
  6. ✅ Confirm response time commitment
  7. ✅ Set up W-9 for 1099 reporting
  8. ✅ Agree on invoicing process (net-15 or net-30, digital invoices)

Preventive Maintenance Schedule

Preventive maintenance reduces emergency calls by 40-60% and extends property life. Here's a recommended schedule:

Quarterly

  • HVAC filter replacement
  • Smoke/CO detector battery check
  • Pest inspection
  • Gutter cleaning (spring and fall)

Semi-Annual

  • HVAC system tune-up (spring for A/C, fall for heating)
  • Water heater flush
  • Exterior inspection (siding, foundation, roof visual)
  • Landscaping assessment

Annual

  • Full property inspection (interior + exterior)
  • Roof inspection
  • Plumbing inspection (check for leaks, water pressure, drain speed)
  • Electrical panel inspection
  • Appliance condition assessment
  • Safety device testing (smoke detectors, CO detectors, fire extinguishers)
  • Dryer vent cleaning

Maintenance Cost Control

Maintenance is the biggest variable cost in PM. Here's how to control it without sacrificing quality:

  1. Negotiate flat rates for common tasks. "Toilet repair: $150 flat. Faucet replacement: $200 flat." Eliminates hourly billing surprises.
  2. Require owner reserves — $500-$1,000 minimum per property. You never want to front costs or delay repairs because an owner is slow to fund.
  3. Set approval thresholds — handle anything under $300-500 without owner approval. This speeds resolution and reduces your coordination time.
  4. Track cost per unit — know your average annual maintenance spend per door. Industry average: $1,500-$3,000/unit/year for residential.
  5. Bundle work — if a handyman is at a property for one issue, have them check for other reported issues too. One trip = one trip charge.
  6. Invest in preventive maintenance — a $150 HVAC tune-up prevents a $3,000 system failure. Always.

Emergency After-Hours Protocol

Emergencies don't wait for business hours. Your protocol:

  1. Answering service or dedicated phone line — tenants call one number, 24/7
  2. Decision tree for the person answering: Is it an actual emergency? (Gas leak, flood, fire = yes. Dripping faucet at 11pm = no.)
  3. Emergency vendor list with 24/7 contacts for: plumber, electrician, locksmith, water damage restoration, glass/board-up
  4. Authorization — your team is pre-authorized to spend up to $500 on emergencies without owner approval
  5. Documentation — photos, timestamps, vendor invoices for every emergency
  6. Owner notification — within 12 hours of any emergency, the owner gets a call or email with what happened, what was done, and the cost
