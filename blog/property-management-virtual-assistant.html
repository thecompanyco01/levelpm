Property Management Virtual Assistant: Complete Hiring Guide [2026]

How to hire, train, and manage a VA for your PM company. Save 20+ hours/week and scale without adding expensive local staff.

Home → Blog → PM Virtual Assistant Guide

Hiring a virtual assistant is the single highest-leverage move for PM companies managing 50-200 doors. A good VA at $6-12/hour can handle tasks that would cost you $25-40/hour locally. That's 3-5x cost savings — and it frees you to focus on owner acquisition, the #1 growth lever for any PM company.

Tasks You Should Delegate to a VA

Tier 1: Delegate Immediately (Low Risk, High Time Savings)

  • Tenant communication — Responding to non-emergency maintenance requests, answering questions about lease terms, sending reminders
  • Listing management — Posting vacancies to Zillow, Apartments.com, Craigslist, Facebook Marketplace; updating photos and descriptions
  • Application processing — Collecting applications, initiating background/credit checks, following up with applicants
  • Owner reporting — Preparing monthly owner statements, compiling maintenance logs, generating financial summaries
  • Rent collection follow-up — Sending late payment reminders, calling tenants about overdue rent, documenting communication
  • Data entry — Entering invoices into PM software, updating tenant records, logging maintenance requests

Tier 2: Delegate After Training (Medium Risk)

  • Maintenance coordination — Dispatching vendors, getting quotes, scheduling repairs, following up on work orders
  • Lease preparation — Drafting leases using your templates, sending for e-signature, filing executed documents
  • Move-in/move-out coordination — Scheduling inspections, coordinating key handoffs, processing security deposit returns
  • Bookkeeping support — Reconciling accounts, categorizing expenses, preparing tax documents
  • Marketing — Social media management, blog writing, email newsletters, review management

Tier 3: Keep In-House (High Risk / Requires Licensing)

  • Lease negotiations — May require a real estate license in your state
  • Eviction decisions — Legal implications require your judgment
  • Owner acquisition/sales — Building relationships is personal
  • Property inspections — Require physical presence
  • Emergency decisions — Judgment calls on safety and spending

How to Train Your VA: The SOP Method

The #1 reason VAs fail is bad training. Here's how to set them up for success:

  1. Document every process as an SOP — Before your VA starts, write step-by-step instructions for every task they'll handle. Include screenshots, video walkthroughs, and decision trees.
  2. Create a 2-week onboarding plan
    • Days 1-3: Software training (your PM platform, email, communication tools)
    • Days 4-7: Shadow you on calls, watch you process requests, study SOPs
    • Days 8-10: Handle tasks with your review before sending
    • Days 11-14: Independent work with daily check-ins
  3. Set clear KPIs from day one
    • Response time to tenant messages: under 2 hours
    • Listing turnaround: new vacancy listed within 24 hours
    • Data accuracy: 99%+ on data entry
    • Owner reports: delivered by the 10th of each month
  4. Daily standups for the first month — 15 minutes at the start of their shift. Review yesterday's work, clarify today's priorities, address questions.
  5. Weekly 1-on-1s after month 1 — Feedback, process improvements, career development. Invest in them and they'll stay.

Tools Your VA Needs Access To

  • PM Software: AppFolio, Buildium, RentManager, or your platform
  • Communication: Slack, Microsoft Teams, or Google Chat
  • Phone system: Google Voice, RingCentral, or Grasshopper (get a local number)
  • Email: Company email address (never personal)
  • Password manager: LastPass, 1Password (never share passwords directly)
  • Time tracking: Hubstaff, Time Doctor, or Clockify
  • Video calls: Zoom or Google Meet for training and meetings
  • Document storage: Google Drive or Dropbox for SOPs and templates

When to Hire Your First VA

The sweet spot for your first VA hire:

  • 50+ doors under management — Below this, you can usually handle everything yourself
  • You're spending 15+ hours/week on admin tasks — That's time not spent on growth
  • Response times are slipping — If tenants wait more than a business day for non-emergency responses
  • You're turning down new owners — The most expensive cost is the deals you can't take
