What Is a Property Management Agreement?

A property management agreement (PMA) is a legally binding contract between a property owner and a management company. It defines the scope of services, compensation, responsibilities, and the terms under which the relationship operates.

Think of it as your business's operating manual for each client relationship. A well-crafted PMA does three things:

  1. Sets clear expectations — what you will and won't do
  2. Protects your revenue — fee structures, payment terms, and reserve requirements
  3. Provides an exit strategy — how either party can end the relationship cleanly
💡 Pro tip: Most PM companies use a generic agreement they found online and never update it. That's how you end up managing a 40-unit complex for the same fee as a single-family home. Your agreement should be a living document that evolves as your business scales.

12 Essential Clauses Every Agreement Needs

1. Scope of Services

The most important clause. List EXACTLY what's included: tenant screening, rent collection, maintenance coordination, inspections, financial reporting, lease execution. Anything not listed = not included. This prevents scope creep.

Include: Frequency of inspections, reporting schedule, maintenance authorization thresholds, after-hours emergency handling.

2. Management Fee Structure

Define your fee as a percentage of collected rent (typical: 8-12%) or a flat monthly fee. Specify: Is it based on gross rent collected or scheduled rent? When is it deducted? What happens during vacancies?

3. Additional Fees

Beyond the base management fee, outline all additional revenue streams:

  • Leasing/placement fee (typically 50-100% of first month's rent)
  • Lease renewal fee ($150-300)
  • Maintenance markup (10-20% on vendor invoices)
  • Eviction coordination fee ($200-500)
  • Setup/onboarding fee ($200-500 per unit)

4. Owner's Responsibilities

Property owners have obligations too. Spell them out: maintaining adequate insurance, funding reserves, complying with fair housing laws, providing access to the property, timely responses to capital expenditure requests.

5. Maintenance Authorization Threshold

Set a dollar amount (typically $300-500) below which you can authorize repairs without owner approval. Above that threshold, you need written approval. For emergencies (burst pipe, gas leak), you can act immediately regardless of amount.

6. Trust Account & Financial Terms

Detail how funds are held (trust/escrow account), when owner distributions are made (typically 10th-15th of month), minimum reserve requirements ($500-1,000 per unit), and how interest is handled.

7. Insurance Requirements

Require the owner to maintain adequate property insurance and name your company as an additional insured. Specify minimum coverage amounts and proof of insurance timeline.

8. Term & Renewal

Most PMAs run 1-2 years with auto-renewal. Include the initial term, auto-renewal period (typically 1 year), and how much notice is required to prevent renewal (30-90 days).

9. Termination Clause

Both parties should have the right to terminate with proper notice (30-90 days). Define what happens to active leases, security deposits, reserved funds, and outstanding maintenance upon termination.

10. Indemnification & Liability

Protect your company from liability arising from owner negligence, property defects, or third-party claims. Include mutual indemnification language and limitation of liability.

11. Dispute Resolution

Specify how disputes are handled: mediation first, then arbitration or litigation. Include the governing state law and jurisdiction. Arbitration is generally faster and cheaper than court.

12. Authority to Act

Explicitly grant yourself the authority to: sign leases on behalf of the owner, hire/fire vendors, serve legal notices, and represent the owner in tenant matters. Without this, you'll constantly need owner signatures.

Termination Clauses That Protect You

The termination clause is where most PMs get burned. Here's what yours should include:

For-Cause Termination

Either party can terminate immediately if the other party:

  • Materially breaches the agreement and fails to cure within 30 days of written notice
  • Files for bankruptcy or becomes insolvent
  • Engages in illegal activity related to the property
  • Fails to maintain required insurance

Without-Cause Termination

Either party can terminate with 60-90 days written notice. This is standard and reasonable. Don't agree to 30 days — it's not enough time to transition properties properly.

Transition Requirements

Upon termination, specify that you will:

  • Transfer all tenant files, leases, and records within 30 days
  • Provide a final accounting within 60 days
  • Transfer security deposits to owner or new manager
  • Return reserve funds minus any outstanding obligations
  • Cooperate with the incoming manager for a smooth transition
⚠️ Warning: Always include an early termination fee. If an owner signs a 1-year agreement and terminates after 3 months, you've lost 9 months of expected revenue plus all the onboarding costs. A fee of 2-3 months' management fees is standard and enforceable in most states.

7 Costly Mistakes PMs Make in Agreements

  1. Using a generic template without customization — Every state has different requirements. A California PMA won't work in Texas.
  2. Not defining scope clearly enough — "Full-service management" means different things to different people. Be specific.
  3. Forgetting the maintenance markup disclosure — Some states require you to disclose vendor markups. Not doing so can cost your license.
  4. No minimum reserve requirement — Without a reserve, you'll be advancing money for repairs. Require $500-1,000 per unit minimum.
  5. Allowing verbal modifications — Include a clause requiring all modifications in writing. Verbal agreements lead to disputes.
  6. Not addressing multi-property discounts — If an owner has 20 units and you agreed to 8%, make sure the agreement covers what happens if they sell half.
  7. Ignoring technology and data clauses — Who owns the tenant data? Who pays for PM software? Address this upfront.

State-Specific Requirements

Property management agreements must comply with state real estate laws. Key variations include:

  • Licensing: Most states require a real estate broker's license to manage properties for others. Some (like California) require a specific PM license.
  • Trust accounts: Nearly all states require client funds in separate trust/escrow accounts. Commingling is a license-revocation offense.
  • Disclosures: Many states require specific disclosures about fees, conflicts of interest, and agency relationships.
  • Fair housing: Federal fair housing laws apply everywhere, but some states and cities have additional protected classes.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long should a property management agreement be?

A thorough PMA is typically 8-15 pages. Don't worry about length — worry about completeness. A short agreement that misses critical clauses will cost you far more in disputes than a longer, comprehensive one.

Can I use the same agreement for residential and commercial properties?

No. Commercial management involves different responsibilities (CAM charges, triple-net leases, tenant improvements) that require a separate agreement. Use a residential PMA for residential and create a separate commercial version.

Should I have a lawyer review my agreement?

Absolutely. Invest $500-1,500 in having a real estate attorney in your state review your template. It's a one-time cost that protects your business for years. Update the review whenever state laws change.

What's the standard notice period for termination?

60-90 days is standard in the industry. Some states have minimum notice requirements. Never agree to less than 30 days — it's not enough time for a proper transition.
