1. NARPM Designation Courses

The National Association of Residential Property Managers offers the industry's most recognized credentials: RMP (Residential Management Professional) and MPM (Master Property Manager). Their online courses cover leasing, maintenance coordination, accounting, and fair housing.

Pros: Industry-recognized designations, peer networking, continuing education credits.

Cons: Must be a NARPM member ($200+/yr), courses feel dated, heavy on association culture.

Best for: Property managers who want credibility with property owners and a professional network.

2. IREM CPM Certification Prep

The Certified Property Manager (CPM) from IREM is the gold standard in commercial and multifamily management. The online prep covers financial analysis, asset management, maintenance planning, and risk management.

Pros: Most respected PM credential globally, deep curriculum, opens doors to large management companies.

Cons: Expensive ($3K–$5K total), takes 12–18 months, requires experience prerequisites.

Best for: Career property managers targeting commercial/multifamily roles or management company leadership.

3. Udemy Property Management Courses

Udemy has 50+ property management courses, with the most popular ones covering PM fundamentals, tenant screening, lease agreements, and marketing for landlords. Prices drop to $15–$20 during frequent sales.

Pros: Cheap, self-paced, wide variety of instructors and topics.

Cons: Quality varies wildly, no industry recognition, some courses are outdated.

Best for: Complete beginners who want to understand the basics before committing to the industry.

4. Coursera Real Estate Management Specializations

Universities like Columbia and the University of Florida offer real estate management programs on Coursera. These cover finance, investment analysis, and property operations with an academic rigor you won't find elsewhere online.

Pros: University-backed curriculum, free to audit, structured learning path.

Cons: Heavy on theory, light on day-to-day PM operations, not PM-specific.

Best for: People who want a theoretical foundation alongside practical PM training.

6. Buildium Academy

If you use Buildium software, their built-in academy offers free training on both the platform and general PM best practices. Covers tenant screening, lease management, accounting workflows, and maintenance coordination within Buildium.

Pros: Free, software-specific, practical for Buildium users.

Cons: Tied to Buildium ecosystem, not useful if you use other software.

7. AppFolio University

Similar to Buildium's offering, AppFolio University provides free training for AppFolio users. Includes webinars, guides, and certification tracks for their platform.

Pros: Free, practical, regularly updated.

Cons: AppFolio-specific, marketing-heavy.

8. BiggerPockets Real Estate Bootcamp

BiggerPockets is primarily an investor community, but their bootcamp and forums contain some of the most practical PM content online. The Pro membership ($39/mo) unlocks calculators, market data, and expert workshops.

Pros: Massive community, real-world advice from active PMs, good calculators.

Cons: Investor-focused (not PM company-focused), forums can be overwhelming.

9. DoorGrow Academy

DoorGrow specializes in helping PM companies grow their door count. Their academy combines online courses with coaching calls. Jason Hull's framework for growing from 0 to 1,000+ doors is well-regarded in the industry.

Pros: Growth-focused (not just operations), active coaching, PM-specific community.

Cons: Expensive ($500+/mo), long-term commitment expected, aggressive sales.

10. LinkedIn Learning PM Courses

LinkedIn Learning offers general business and real estate courses that are tangentially useful for PMs — negotiation, accounting fundamentals, customer service, project management.

Pros: Professional skills that complement PM-specific training, certificates for LinkedIn profile.

Cons: Not PM-specific, generic business content.

11. State Real Estate Schools (Online)

Most states require a real estate license or PM-specific license. Online real estate schools (like Kaplan, The CE Shop, Colibri) offer state-approved pre-licensing courses that you can complete online.

Pros: Required for legal compliance, available online in most states.

Cons: Focused on passing the exam (not actual PM skills), state-specific.

12. YouTube PM Training (Free)

Several PM professionals share genuine operational knowledge on YouTube for free. Channels worth watching include those covering day-to-day PM operations, tenant relations, and scaling strategies.

Pros: Completely free, diverse perspectives, visual learning.

Cons: Unstructured, quality varies, easy to waste time watching without acting.

How to Choose the Right Online PM Training

If you're brand new to property management:

Start with free resources — Udemy (on sale), YouTube, and Buildium/AppFolio academies. Get a feel for the industry before investing in expensive programs. Then get your state license if required.

If you're managing 1–50 doors and want to grow:

Focus on operational training. Download our free SOPs for immediate systems you can implement. Consider NARPM courses for credibility with property owners.

If you're scaling past 50 doors:

You don't need more theory — you need systems. The PM Scaling Kit gives you hiring playbooks, financial frameworks, and growth SOPs specifically for the 50-to-500 door transition.

If you want career credentials:

IREM CPM is the gold standard for commercial/multifamily. NARPM RMP/MPM for residential. Both are worth the investment if you're building a long-term PM career.

Free vs. Paid PM Training: Is It Worth Paying?

Free training is good enough to get started. You can learn PM fundamentals, pass your licensing exam, and manage your first few properties using only free resources.

Paid training becomes worth it when you're scaling. The difference between managing 30 doors and 300 doors isn't knowledge — it's systems, processes, and operational discipline. That's where structured paid programs and operational toolkits pay for themselves many times over.
