Detailed Reviews

1. IREM CPM® Certification Program

Price: $5,000–$10,000 (courses + membership + exam fees)
Duration: 12–18 months
Format: Mix of online and in-person courses

The CPM® is the gold standard — and it charges gold-standard prices. Eight courses covering finance, marketing, maintenance, risk management, and ethics. You also need 36 months of qualifying experience and a management plan project.

Pros: Globally recognized. Comprehensive curriculum. Forces structured learning. Opens doors in commercial/institutional PM.

Cons: Extremely expensive. 12-18 month commitment. Much of the content is academic, not practical. Requires IREM membership ($400+/year ongoing). Designed for large portfolio/commercial managers, not residential PM company owners.

Our take: Worth it if you manage commercial properties or want institutional clients. Overkill and overpriced for residential PM company owners trying to scale from 50 to 200 doors.

2. NARPM® Designations (RMP®, MPM®)

Price: $500–$2,000 (courses + membership + designation fees)
Duration: Ongoing (experience-based milestones)
Format: Online courses + annual conference

NARPM's designations are specifically for residential property managers. The RMP® (Residential Management Professional) requires 100+ doors and 2 years experience. The MPM® (Master Property Manager) requires 500+ doors and 5 years.

Pros: Residential-focused (unlike IREM). Networking through local chapters. Industry-specific knowledge.

Cons: Courses feel dated. Association charges for everything — membership, courses, conference, designation, CE. The actual training content is thin compared to what you pay. Heavy emphasis on association participation vs. practical skill building.

Our take: The NARPM network has value. The courses themselves? You can learn more from a good blog post. Consider joining for networking but don't expect the courses to transform your business.

3. NAA CAM (Certified Apartment Manager)

Price: $1,500–$3,000
Duration: 3–6 months
Format: Online modules + proctored exam

The CAM is the most popular certification for apartment community managers. Seven modules covering operations, finance, marketing, legal, maintenance, risk, and HR. 120-question exam with 70% passing score.

Pros: Well-structured curriculum. Recognized by apartment industry employers. Relatively fast (3-6 months). Covers practical on-site management skills.

Cons: Designed for site managers (employees), not PM company owners. Doesn't teach business growth, owner acquisition, or scaling. You're learning to manage an apartment community, not build a PM business.

Our take: Best option if you're an on-site apartment community manager. Not relevant if you own a PM company managing single-family or small multi-family.

4. Udemy Property Management Courses

Price: $15–$50 (often on sale for $10-15)
Duration: 2–10 hours
Format: Video on-demand

Udemy has dozens of PM courses. Most are created by individual instructors with varying quality. The top-rated ones cover basics: tenant screening, lease agreements, maintenance management, financial tracking.

Pros: Cheap. Accessible. Good for absolute basics. 30-day refund policy.

Cons: Extremely shallow. No templates or SOPs you can actually use. Generic advice that applies to any business. No community or support. Many courses are outdated. You get what you pay for.

Our take: Fine for someone who's never managed a property and wants to understand the basics in an afternoon. Useless for anyone already managing properties who wants to grow.

5. Coursera — Property Management (UC Irvine)

Price: $49/month (Coursera Plus) or audit for free
Duration: 4-6 weeks
Format: Video lectures + assignments + certificate

UC Irvine's Property Management specialization on Coursera covers fundamentals from an academic perspective. Good production quality, structured learning path, and you get a university-branded certificate.

Pros: University credential. Well-produced content. Can audit for free. Structured assignments force engagement.

Cons: Academic, not practical. No SOPs or templates you can implement. Taught by professors, not practitioners. The certificate has minimal industry recognition (it's Coursera, not IREM/NARPM).

Our take: Good if you want to understand PM theory. But theory doesn't fill vacancies or acquire owner clients.

What Most Courses Get Wrong

Here's the dirty secret of PM education: most courses teach you to manage properties, not to build a property management business.

There's a massive difference between:

  • "Here's how a lease agreement works" (every course teaches this)
  • "Here's a repeatable system to acquire 5 new owners per month" (almost no course teaches this)

If you're managing properties for others, you already know the basics. What you actually need is:

  1. SOPs that let your team handle operations without you
  2. Templates for owner reporting, tenant screening, inspections that are professional and consistent
  3. Growth systems for acquiring new owner clients systematically
  4. Financial frameworks for pricing, tracking KPIs, and improving profitability
