Property Management RFP Template: How to Write & Respond to Win

Complete RFP template with scoring criteria for property owners, HOAs, and PM companies.

A Request for Proposal (RFP) is how property owners, HOAs, and real estate investors formally solicit bids from property management companies. Whether you're issuing an RFP or responding to one, having the right template saves weeks of back-and-forth and ensures you compare vendors on what actually matters.

Who Needs a Property Management RFP?

  • HOA boards selecting or replacing a management company
  • Commercial property owners with multi-tenant buildings
  • Real estate investors with 10+ unit portfolios
  • Institutional owners (REITs, pension funds, family offices)
  • PM companies responding to RFPs to win new business

Property Management RFP Template (Issuer Side)

Section 1: Company Overview & Property Details

  • Organization name and contact information
  • Number and type of properties/units to be managed
  • Current management situation (self-managed, switching PMs, new construction)
  • Property locations, ages, and condition
  • Current occupancy rates and average rents
  • Special requirements (affordable housing compliance, commercial leasing, etc.)

Section 2: Scope of Services Required

  • Tenant screening and placement
  • Rent collection and delinquency management
  • Maintenance coordination (routine, preventive, emergency)
  • Financial reporting (frequency, format, detail level)
  • Lease administration (renewals, terminations)
  • Compliance management (fair housing, local regulations)
  • Tenant communications and portal access
  • Move-in/move-out inspections
  • Capital improvement oversight
  • Insurance claims management

Section 3: Vendor Qualifications Required

  • Years in business and portfolio size
  • Licenses and certifications (CPM, RMP, MPM)
  • Insurance coverage (E&O, general liability minimums)
  • Technology stack (tenant portal, maintenance tracking, accounting)
  • Staff size and organizational structure
  • References from similar-sized portfolios
  • Transition plan and timeline

Section 4: Pricing Structure Request

  • Monthly management fee (% or flat rate)
  • Leasing/tenant placement fee
  • Lease renewal fee
  • Maintenance markup policy
  • Additional fees (inspections, evictions, after-hours, etc.)
  • Minimum contract term
  • Early termination provisions

Section 5: Submission Requirements

  • Deadline for proposals
  • Format requirements
  • Questions/clarifications deadline
  • Evaluation criteria and weighting
  • Interview/presentation schedule
  • Expected decision date
  • Anticipated start date

How to Respond to a Property Management RFP (PM Company Side)

If you're a PM company responding to RFPs, here's how to stand out:

1. Address Every Section Explicitly

Don't skip sections or give generic answers. Mirror the RFP structure in your response so evaluators can score you easily.

2. Lead with Results, Not Capabilities

Instead of "We offer tenant screening," say: "Our screening process has reduced eviction rates by 40% across our portfolio of 2,300 units."

3. Include a Detailed Transition Plan

This is where most competitors drop the ball. Include a week-by-week transition timeline with specific milestones, tenant communication templates, and a dedicated transition manager.

4. Show Your Technology

Include screenshots of your tenant portal, owner dashboard, and maintenance tracking system. Visual proof beats descriptions.

5. Price Transparently

List every possible fee upfront. Companies that hide fees in the fine print lose on trust.

Pro tip: Include a "Total Cost of Management" table showing the estimated annual cost based on the RFP's property details. Make it easy for the evaluator to compare you apples-to-apples with competitors.

Common RFP Mistakes to Avoid

If You're Issuing the RFP:

  • Being too vague about scope — PMs can't price what they can't define
  • Only evaluating on price — the cheapest PM is rarely the best
  • Not providing enough property details — forces PMs to guess on pricing
  • Setting unrealistic timelines — give PMs at least 2-3 weeks to respond

If You're Responding to the RFP:

  • Using a boilerplate response — customize for each property type
  • Ignoring the scoring criteria — weight your response accordingly
  • Missing the deadline — an immediate disqualification
  • Not asking clarifying questions — shows lack of thoroughness
