2. Setting Up Your Management System

Before your first tenant moves in, you need five foundational systems:

  1. Accounting system — Track every dollar in and out per property. QuickBooks or your PM software works.
  2. Lease template — State-specific, attorney-reviewed. Cover pets, maintenance responsibilities, and lease violations clearly.
  3. Tenant communication channel — One official channel (tenant portal, email, or app). Not texts to your personal phone.
  4. Maintenance request system — Written requests with timestamps. Never verbal-only.
  5. Document storage — Leases, inspection photos, receipts, correspondence. Cloud-based, organized by property and year.
Pro tip: Set these up BEFORE you need them. The worst time to build a maintenance triage system is when a pipe bursts at 2am.

3. Tenant Screening & Placement

Bad tenants are the #1 profit killer in property management. A thorough screening process is non-negotiable.

The 5-Point Screening Checklist

  1. Credit check — Minimum 620 score for most markets. Look at payment history, not just the number.
  2. Income verification — 3x monthly rent minimum. Verify with pay stubs (last 2 months) and employer contact.
  3. Rental history — Call previous landlords (at least 2). Ask: "Would you rent to them again?"
  4. Background check — Criminal + eviction history. Follow Fair Housing laws — apply consistently to all applicants.
  5. Employment verification — Confirm employment, position, and tenure directly with employer.

4. Rent Collection That Works

The goal: collect 98%+ of rent on time, every month. Here's how the best managers achieve it:

  • Online payments only. No checks, no cash, no excuses about "it's in the mail." Set up ACH auto-pay from day one.
  • Late fee enforcement. Grace period (3-5 days), then automatic late fee. Never waive it — inconsistency creates problems.
  • Early communication. Rent reminder 3 days before due date. It's not nagging, it's professional.
  • Clear lease terms. Due date, late fee amount, and eviction timeline should be crystal clear in the lease.

6. Financial Management & Reporting

Every rental property should produce monthly financials that answer three questions:

  1. How much cash did this property generate? (Net Operating Income)
  2. What were the major expenses? (Categorized by maintenance, capex, admin, vacancy)
  3. Is this property meeting my return target? (Cash-on-cash return)

7. Legal Compliance Essentials

Property management is heavily regulated. Ignorance isn't a defense. These are the areas where landlords get into trouble most often:

  • Fair Housing Act — Cannot discriminate based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin, familial status, or disability. Apply the same screening criteria to every applicant.
  • Security deposit laws — Maximum amounts and return timelines vary by state. Know your state's rules exactly.
  • Habitability standards — You must provide safe, habitable conditions: working plumbing, heating, electrical, and structural integrity.
  • Entry notice requirements — Most states require 24-48 hours notice before entering a unit (except emergencies).
  • Eviction procedures — Follow your state's exact process. Shortcuts lead to lawsuits.

10. Top 10 Mistakes to Avoid

  1. No written lease. Verbal agreements are unenforceable. Always use a state-specific written lease.
  2. Skipping tenant screening. One bad tenant can cost $5,000-$30,000 in damages, lost rent, and legal fees.
  3. Not documenting condition. Take 100+ timestamped photos at every move-in and move-out.
  4. Delayed maintenance. A $150 leak becomes a $15,000 mold problem. Fix issues fast.
  5. Inconsistent rule enforcement. Either enforce late fees for everyone or no one. Inconsistency invites discrimination claims.
  6. No emergency fund. Keep 3-6 months of expenses per property in reserve.
  7. DIY evictions. Always hire an eviction attorney. The $500-$1,500 fee is cheaper than doing it wrong.
  8. Mixing personal and rental funds. Separate bank accounts for each property (or at minimum, one for all rentals).
  9. Ignoring state laws. Security deposit rules, notice requirements, and habitability standards vary wildly by state.
  10. Not raising rent. Below-market rent loses you thousands annually. Raise to market rate at each renewal.
