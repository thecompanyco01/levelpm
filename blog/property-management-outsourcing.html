Property Management Outsourcing: What to Delegate & When to Hire

March 7, 2026 · 12 min read · By LevelPM

The property managers who scale fastest aren't the ones who do everything themselves — they're the ones who know what to outsource. Whether you're managing 20 doors or 200, outsourcing the right functions lets you grow your portfolio without proportionally growing your headcount or overhead.

But outsourcing wrong is worse than not outsourcing at all. Hand off the wrong things, or hand them to the wrong people, and you'll spend more time managing vendors than you saved. This guide shows you exactly what to outsource, when, and how to find reliable partners.

The Outsourcing Decision Matrix

Not everything should be outsourced. Use this framework to decide:

VolumeInconsistent or seasonal demandSteady daily volume justifying a full-time role
ExpertiseRequires specialized skills you don't haveYou can train someone faster than managing a vendor

What to Outsource (and When)

1. Maintenance & Repairs

When to outsource: Always (for most PM companies under 300 doors)

Unless you have enough volume to justify a full-time maintenance technician (typically 150-200+ doors), outsource maintenance to a vetted vendor network.

  • Build a vendor list: 2-3 vendors per trade (plumbing, electrical, HVAC, general handyman, appliance repair)
  • Negotiate rates: Volume commitments get you 10-20% discounts. "We'll send you all our plumbing calls" is powerful leverage.
  • Set response time SLAs: Emergency (2 hours), urgent (24 hours), routine (72 hours) — put it in writing
  • Use work order software: Propertyware, AppFolio, or even a simple Google Form → Sheet pipeline. Never manage maintenance via text messages.
When to bring maintenance in-house: At ~200 doors, a full-time maintenance tech ($45-65K/year) typically costs less than outsourced vendor calls. The breakeven is roughly when you're spending $5K+/month on routine maintenance vendors.

2. Accounting & Bookkeeping

When to outsource: From day one until you hit 200+ doors

Property management accounting is specialized — trust accounting, owner disbursements, 1099s, and reconciliations require expertise that general bookkeepers often lack.

  • Cost: $500-1,500/month for a specialized PM bookkeeper (remote)
  • What to outsource: Monthly reconciliation, owner statements, AP/AR, trust account management, year-end tax preparation
  • What to keep: Rent collection (your PMS handles this), invoice approval, budget decisions
  • Where to find: NARPM vendor directory, PM-specific accounting firms (Fourandhalf, PM Bookkeeper), remote accountants on Upwork with PM experience

3. Leasing & Showings

When to outsource: When leasing volume exceeds your team's capacity

  • Options: Showing agents (ShowMojo, Rently for self-showings), licensed leasing agents on commission, virtual assistants for application processing
  • Cost: $200-500 per lease (commission to showing agent) or $15-30/hour for a VA handling applications
  • Self-showing technology: Smart lockboxes (Rently, Codebox) let prospects tour properties without an agent. Conversion rates are lower but cost per showing drops to near zero.

4. Virtual Assistants (VAs)

When to outsource: At 30-50 doors

VAs are the most cost-effective way to scale PM operations. A trained PM VA ($5-12/hour) can handle:

  • Tenant communication (responding to maintenance requests, general inquiries)
  • Lease renewal processing
  • Application screening coordination
  • Vendor scheduling and coordination
  • Move-in/move-out documentation
  • Data entry and reporting
VA hiring tip: Don't hire a general VA and hope they figure out PM. Hire from agencies that specialize in property management VAs (Anequim, MyOutDesk, Rocket Station). They come pre-trained on common PM software.

5. Marketing & Lead Generation

When to outsource: When you need owner leads but can't justify a full-time marketer

  • SEO & content: $1,000-3,000/month for a PM-focused content agency
  • Google Ads: $500-2,000/month in ad spend + $500-1,000/month for management
  • Social media: $300-800/month for consistent posting and engagement
  • Website: One-time $2,000-5,000 for a professional PM website

6. Legal

When to outsource: Always (unless you're 500+ doors with in-house counsel)

  • Eviction filings and court appearances
  • Lease agreement updates and compliance review
  • Fair Housing compliance audits
  • Dispute resolution

Find an attorney who specializes in landlord-tenant law in your state. Retainer agreements ($500-1,500/month) are cost-effective for companies doing regular evictions.

How to Find and Vet Outsourcing Partners

Finding Vendors

  1. NARPM vendor directory: Pre-vetted vendors who understand PM
  2. Local PM association meetups: Ask other PMs who they use
  3. Online marketplaces: Upwork (VAs, bookkeepers), Thumbtack (maintenance), Angi (contractors)
  4. PM conferences: PM Grow, NARPM Broker/Owner — vendor halls are goldmines

Vetting Process

  • Check insurance and licensing (especially for maintenance vendors)
  • Ask for 3 PM company references — call them
  • Start with a small test project before committing to a contract
  • Review their response time and communication style during the sales process — it only gets worse after they sign you
  • Negotiate 30-day out clauses so you can switch if quality drops

Outsourcing Pitfalls to Avoid

  1. Outsourcing owner relations: Never. Your relationship with property owners is your core business. If they feel like they're talking to a stranger, they'll leave.
  2. No quality checks: Outsourcing doesn't mean set-and-forget. Audit vendor work monthly. Review VA communication logs. Check bookkeeping accuracy.
  3. Single vendor dependency: Always have backup vendors for critical functions. If your only plumber ghosts you on a Friday night, you need a Plan B.
  4. Cheapest bid wins: A $30/hour handyman who fixes it right the first time is cheaper than a $15/hour one who comes back three times.
  5. No SOPs: If you can't clearly document what you want done, you can't outsource it effectively. Write the SOP first, then delegate.

Calculating Your Outsourcing ROI

The math is simple: if outsourcing a function costs less than doing it in-house AND frees you to acquire more doors, it's a no-brainer.

Example: Outsourcing Admin to a VA

  • VA cost: $10/hour × 20 hours/week = $800/month
  • Your time saved: 20 hours/week
  • Value of your time: If you use those 20 hours to acquire 2 new doors/month at $150/door management fee = $300/month recurring
  • ROI: Within 3 months, the new management fees exceed the VA cost permanently

Bottom Line

Outsourcing is how small PM companies compete with large ones. You don't need 50 employees to manage 500 doors — you need the right partners, clear SOPs, and a framework for knowing what to delegate and what to own.

Start by outsourcing what you're worst at or what takes the most time for the least value. Then systematically hand off more as you grow. The goal isn't to do less — it's to focus your time on the activities that grow your portfolio: owner acquisition, client retention, and strategic decisions.
