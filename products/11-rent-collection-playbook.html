Rent Collection Playbook

The complete automated workflow for collecting rent on time, every time — from payment setup through eviction escalation.

Why This Matters
At 100 doors with average rent of $1,400/month, every 1% improvement in on-time collection equals $16,800 per year in avoided delinquency. Property managers who follow a structured collection process achieve 97%+ on-time rates vs. the industry average of 91%.

1. Payment Method Setup Guide

Your payment infrastructure determines your collection rate. The goal: make it as easy as possible for tenants to pay on time, and as automated as possible for your team to track.

ACH / Direct Bank Transfer (Target: 70%+ of Tenants)

ACH is your best friend. It's the cheapest processing method and enables auto-pay. Push every tenant toward ACH enrollment at lease signing.

  • Processing cost: $0.25–$1.50 per transaction (vs. 2.9% for credit cards)
  • Settlement time: 3–5 business days
  • NSF risk: Have a clear NSF fee policy ($25–$50) and require two consecutive successful payments before allowing auto-pay
  • Setup process: Collect routing number, account number, and signed ACH authorization form at lease signing
Pro Tip
Offer a $25 discount on the first month's rent for tenants who enroll in ACH auto-pay. The lifetime value of automated payments far exceeds the one-time discount.

Credit/Debit Card Payments

  • Processing cost: 2.5%–3.5% per transaction — pass this to the tenant as a "convenience fee"
  • Settlement time: 1–2 business days
  • Policy: Accept credit cards but require tenant to pay the convenience fee. Put this in the lease addendum.
  • Chargebacks: Rare for rent but document everything. Keep signed lease and payment authorization on file.

Check / Money Order Policy

  • Strongly discourage — checks slow down your process and increase manual work
  • If accepted: Only cashier's checks or money orders after any bounced check. No personal checks after NSF.
  • Drop-off location: If you allow physical payments, provide a secure drop box and process daily by 2 PM
  • Mail policy: Payments mailed must be postmarked by the 1st. Late postmarks = late payment.

3. Day-by-Day Late Rent Collection Timeline

This is the core of your collection process. Follow it exactly — consistency is what makes it work. Every tenant gets the same treatment, every time.

Day 1 (1st of Month) — Rent Due
Automated actions: PM software sends payment reminder (if not on auto-pay). Portal shows "Rent Due" status. No manual action needed.
Team action: None. Let the system work.
Day 3 — Friendly Reminder
Automated: Send automated text/email reminder: "Hi [Name], just a friendly reminder that your rent of $[amount] is due. Pay online at [link]. Grace period ends on the 5th."
Team action: None — fully automated.
Day 5 — Grace Period Ends
Automated: Final grace period warning: "Your grace period ends tonight at 11:59 PM. After today, a late fee of $[amount] will be automatically assessed."
Team action: Pull non-payment report. Flag units for Day 6 action.
Day 6 — Late Fee Assessed
Automated: Late fee posted to tenant account. Notification sent: "A late fee of $[amount] has been applied to your account. Your total balance due is $[total]. Pay now at [link]."
Team action: Property manager reviews delinquency list. No personal calls yet — let the system create urgency.
Day 8 — Personal Contact Attempt
Manual: Call the tenant directly. Use this script:
"Hi [Name], this is [PM Name] from [Company]. I'm calling about your rent for [month] — I see it hasn't come through yet and there's a late fee on your account. Is everything okay? I want to help you get this resolved. Can you make the payment today?"
Document: Log the call in your PM software — date, time, who you spoke with, what was said.
Day 10 — Written Notice (Pre-Legal)
Manual: Send a formal late rent notice via email AND certified mail or posted on door. This is NOT the legal notice — it's a firm reminder.
"This letter serves as formal notice that your rent of $[amount] plus late fees of $[fees] totaling $[total] is past due. Please remit payment immediately to avoid further action including legal proceedings."
Team action: Notify owner: "[Property address] — tenant is 10 days delinquent. We are following our standard collection process. Legal notice will be issued on Day 14 if unresolved."
Day 14 — Pay-or-Quit Notice Served
CRITICAL LEGAL STEP: Serve the formal Pay-or-Quit notice (also called "Notice to Pay Rent or Vacate"). This must comply with your state's requirements for:
  • Notice period (3 days in CA/TX, 5 days in FL, 14 days in NY/OR)
  • Delivery method (personal service, posting + mailing, etc.)
  • Content requirements (exact amount owed, where to pay, deadline)
Owner notification: "Pay-or-quit notice served. Tenant has [X] days to pay or we proceed with eviction filing."
Day 17–21 — Pay-or-Quit Expires (varies by state)
Decision point: Did the tenant pay in full?
YES → Accept payment including all late fees. Document everything. Reset.
NO → Proceed to eviction filing. Contact your eviction attorney. Do NOT accept partial payment at this stage (see Partial Payment Policy below).
Day 21–25 — Eviction Filing
Attorney files eviction complaint (unlawful detainer / forcible entry & detainer / summary process — terminology varies by state).
Costs: $300–$1,200 depending on state and attorney.
Owner decision: Confirm owner authorizes eviction and understands costs. Get written approval.
Day 30+ — Court Process
Court hearing scheduled (typically 2–6 weeks after filing). Continue documenting all communication attempts. Do NOT accept partial payments without attorney guidance — it may reset the eviction clock.

Assessment Rules

  1. Automatic, no exceptions. The system assesses late fees. Your team does not waive them. Period.
  2. One waiver per lease term. If a tenant with a perfect payment history has a genuine emergency, you may waive ONE late fee per lease term. Document it. Require a written request.
  3. NSF/bounced payment fee: $25–$50 in addition to late fees. The bounced payment does NOT count as a payment — the rent is still outstanding.
  4. Payment application order: All payments apply to oldest balance first → late fees → current rent. This is critical. Put it in the lease.

Critical
A defective pay-or-quit notice will get your eviction case dismissed. Common errors: wrong amount listed, wrong notice period, improper service method, missing required language. Use attorney-reviewed templates specific to your state. This is not the place to save money.

Notice Content Requirements (Universal)

  • Full legal name(s) of all tenants on the lease
  • Property address (full, including unit number)
  • Exact amount of rent owed (do NOT include late fees in some states)
  • Period for which rent is owed (e.g., "March 2026")
  • Number of days to pay or vacate
  • Where and how to make payment (address, online portal, etc.)
  • Date of notice
  • Signature of property manager or authorized agent
  • Proof of service (signed affidavit of service)

6. Partial Payment Acceptance Policy

This Section Can Make or Break Your Eviction
Accepting partial payment after serving a pay-or-quit notice can reset the eviction clock in many states. This is the #1 mistake that costs PMs thousands of dollars and months of delay.

Policy Rules

StageAccept Partial?Conditions
Day 1–13 (before notice)YES — with signed agreementMust sign a Payment Plan Agreement (see template below). Balance due by specific date. Late fees still apply.
Day 14+ (after notice served)NO — unless attorney approvesAccepting ANY payment may waive your notice and restart the eviction timeline. Consult your attorney.
During eviction proceedingsNEVER without court/attorney guidanceAny acceptance is typically deemed waiver of the eviction action.

If You Must Accept Partial Payment After Notice

  1. Consult your eviction attorney FIRST — confirm it won't waive your notice in your state
  2. Use a "Nonwaiver of Rights" agreement signed by the tenant acknowledging the partial payment does not waive your eviction right
  3. Document everything — amount accepted, date, signed agreement
  4. Some states (CA, for example) do NOT allow you to accept partial payment and continue eviction. Period.

8. Eviction Escalation Decision Tree

Start: Rent is 14+ days late. Pay-or-quit notice period has expired.

Has the tenant communicated at all?
↓ YES
Is the tenant offering a reasonable payment plan (full balance within 30 days)?
↓ YES → Accept with signed Payment Plan Agreement. Monitor weekly.
↓ NO (unreasonable plan or excuses only)
Is the tenant requesting more than 30 days to pay in full?
↓ YES → Decline. Proceed to eviction filing. A 60-day payment plan on a single month's rent signals inability to pay going forward.

Has the tenant communicated at all?

↓ NO (ghosting)
File eviction immediately. Zero-communication delinquency almost never resolves itself. File on Day 15–17 (immediately after notice expires).

Special circumstances to consider before filing:

  • Military service: SCRA (Servicemembers Civil Relief Act) protections apply. Consult attorney.
  • Active COVID/disaster moratorium: Check current local ordinances. Some municipalities still have protections.
  • Section 8/voucher tenant: Housing authority portion should be coming — confirm with HA before filing. Only file on tenant portion if HA is current.
  • Owner instructions: Some owners prefer Cash for Keys over eviction. Discuss with owner before filing.

Cash for Keys: When to Offer

Sometimes it's cheaper to pay a tenant to leave than to evict them. Consider Cash for Keys when:

  • Eviction will take 60+ days in your jurisdiction
  • Total eviction cost (attorney + court + lost rent + turnover) exceeds $3,000
  • Tenant is cooperative but genuinely cannot pay
  • Property will re-rent quickly at equal or higher rate

Standard offer: Waive outstanding balance + $500–$1,500 cash for keys returned, property broom-clean, within 7–14 days. Get it in writing with a specific move-out date and inspection requirements.

10. Month-End Reconciliation Procedures

Last Business Day of Month Checklist

  • Pull delinquency report from PM software — list all units with outstanding balances
  • Verify all payments received match bank deposits (3-way reconciliation: software ↔ bank ↔ trust ledger)
  • Confirm all late fees assessed correctly — no missed assessments, no double-charges
  • Review payment plan compliance — are tenants on plans making their scheduled payments?
  • Update owner statements with delinquency notes for any unit with outstanding balance
  • Calculate and record: Total rent collected / Total rent due = Collection Rate %
  • Reconcile trust account — every dollar must be accounted for by property and tenant
  • Generate and distribute owner disbursements (rent collected minus management fee minus expenses)
  • File completed pay-or-quit notices and any legal correspondence in tenant files
  • Review upcoming lease expirations — do NOT renew tenants with chronic late payment history without owner discussion

12. Collection Agency Referral Criteria

After a tenant has moved out (voluntarily or via eviction) with an outstanding balance, you have two options: write it off or send to collections. Here's when to refer:

Refer to Collections When:

  • Balance owed exceeds $500 (anything less costs more to collect than you'll recover)
  • Tenant has moved out and has no active lease with you
  • You have documented proof of the debt (signed lease, ledger, correspondence)
  • 30 days have passed since move-out with no payment arrangement
  • Tenant is not active military (SCRA protections)

Do NOT Refer When:

  • Balance is under $500 — not worth the collection fee (typically 25–50% of amount collected)
  • You don't have complete documentation (lease, ledger, move-out inspection, notices served)
  • Tenant is disputing the amount and you haven't resolved the dispute
  • Security deposit disposition letter hasn't been sent within the state-required timeframe

Recommended Collection Agency Selection Criteria

  • Specializes in residential tenant debt (not medical or credit card debt)
  • Contingency-based fee structure (they only get paid if they collect — typically 25–40%)
  • Licensed in the state where the tenant now resides
  • Reports to all three credit bureaus
  • Complies with FDCPA (Fair Debt Collection Practices Act)
  • Provides monthly reporting on collection status

Information to Provide to the Collection Agency

  • Tenant full legal name and any known aliases
  • Last known address (current, not the rental property)
  • Social Security Number (from the rental application)
  • Date of birth
  • Phone numbers and email addresses
  • Employer information (if known)
  • Copy of signed lease agreement
  • Complete ledger showing all charges and payments
  • Copies of all notices served
  • Move-out inspection report and photos
  • Security deposit disposition letter
  • Total amount owed (itemized: unpaid rent, late fees, damages beyond deposit, lease break fees)
Final Thought
The best rent collection system is one you never have to think about. Set up the automation, train your team on the timeline, and follow it without exception. Consistency is the #1 predictor of high collection rates. When every tenant knows that Day 6 means a late fee and Day 14 means a legal notice — no exceptions, no excuses — they prioritize your rent over other bills. That's the goal.
