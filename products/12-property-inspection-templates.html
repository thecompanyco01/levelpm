Why Inspections Matter at Scale
At 100+ doors, you can't rely on memory or informal walkthroughs. A missed water leak costs $3,000–$15,000 in remediation. A missed code violation costs $500–$5,000 in fines. Systematic inspections catch $50 problems before they become $5,000 emergencies. This system pays for itself on the first catch.

1. Condition Rating Scale

Use this standardized 5-point scale for every inspection item across all forms. Consistency is essential — train every inspector to use the same definitions.

1 — Excellent
Like new. No wear. No action needed.
2 — Good
Minor wear consistent with age. No action needed.
3 — Fair
Moderate wear. Monitor. Schedule maintenance within 6 months.
4 — Poor
Significant wear/damage. Needs repair within 30 days.
5 — Failed/Urgent
Safety hazard or non-functional. Immediate repair required.

2. Inspection Scheduling & Tenant Notice

No statuteNo specific statute — still provide 24+ hours as best practiceAL, CO, MS

Inspection Scheduling Procedure

  1. 60 days before: Add inspection to calendar. Assign inspector from team.
  2. 14 days before: Send initial notification to tenant (email + portal): "Your annual property inspection is scheduled for the week of [date]. We'll confirm the exact date and time shortly."
  3. 7 days before: Send confirmed date/time and statutory notice: "Your inspection is scheduled for [date] at [time]. Per [State] law, this serves as your [24/48]-hour advance notice. An inspector will need access to all rooms. Estimated duration: 30-45 minutes. You are welcome to be present but it is not required."
  4. Day before: Confirmation reminder via text: "Reminder: Property inspection tomorrow at [time]. Please ensure access to all areas. Questions? Call [number]."
  5. Day of: Inspector arrives on time. Knocks and announces. If no answer and proper notice was given, enter with management key per lease terms.

6. Seasonal Inspection Reminders

Spring (March–May)

  • Inspect roof for winter damage — missing shingles, ice dam effects
  • Clean gutters and downspouts — remove winter debris
  • Test A/C systems — schedule HVAC tune-up before summer
  • Check exterior paint and caulking — schedule touch-ups
  • Inspect sprinkler systems — repair heads, adjust timers
  • Trim trees/shrubs away from structures (6-foot clearance)
  • Check window and door seals — reseal as needed
  • Inspect crawl spaces for moisture after spring rains
  • Test all smoke detectors and CO detectors (replace batteries)
  • Schedule annual interior inspections for May–June

Summer (June–August)

  • Monitor A/C performance — respond quickly to cooling complaints
  • Check for pest activity — ants, wasps, mosquitoes, termites
  • Inspect pool/spa equipment and fencing (if applicable)
  • Water landscape plants during drought conditions
  • Check exterior for peeling paint in sun-exposed areas
  • Ensure dryer vents are clear — fire prevention
  • Inspect exterior drainage — are downspouts directing water away?

Fall (September–November)

  • Schedule furnace tune-up and inspection before heating season
  • Replace HVAC filters
  • Clean gutters — remove leaves before winter
  • Inspect and service fireplaces/chimneys
  • Check weatherstripping on doors and windows
  • Insulate exposed pipes for freeze prevention
  • Test smoke and CO detectors (Daylight Saving Time reminder)
  • Inspect attic insulation
  • Schedule semi-annual drive-by exterior inspections
  • Aerate and overseed lawns (if owner-maintained)

Winter (December–February)

  • Monitor for frozen pipes — send tenant prevention reminders
  • Check furnace operation weekly during cold snaps
  • Inspect for ice dams and icicle buildup
  • Ensure walkways are clear of ice — liability prevention
  • Check for roof leaks after storms
  • Monitor vacant units — keep heat at 55°F minimum to prevent pipe freeze
  • Check emergency supplies (sand/salt at multi-family properties)

7. Report Writing Guidelines

What to Document

  • Be specific: "3-inch water stain on ceiling in SE corner of bedroom 2" — not "ceiling stain"
  • Quantify damage: Include dimensions, count, and severity
  • Note location: Use compass directions, wall positions (N/S/E/W), proximity to fixtures
  • Describe cause if apparent: "Water stain likely from bathroom above" or "damage consistent with pet scratching"
  • Document lease violations clearly: "Unauthorized pet observed (medium-sized dog) — no pet addendum on file"

Photography Standards

  1. Minimum 50 photos per unit for annual inspection (100+ for move-in/move-out)
  2. Wide-angle shot of each room — shows overall condition from doorway
  3. Close-up of every deficiency — with something for scale (ruler, coin, hand)
  4. Timestamp enabled on camera/phone — critical for legal purposes
  5. Photo naming convention: [Address]_[Room]_[Item]_[Date].jpg (e.g., 123Main_Kitchen_Sink_20260315.jpg)
  6. Photo log: Every photo numbered and referenced in the inspection form ("See Photo #23")
  7. Serial numbers: Photograph appliance model/serial numbers and water heater labels

Staffing for Inspections at Scale
1–75 doors: PM or assistant handles all inspections (budget 45 min/unit for interior, 10 min for drive-by).
75–200 doors: Dedicate one team member to inspections 2-3 days/week. Can complete 6-8 interior inspections per day.
200+ doors: Full-time inspector role. One inspector can handle ~400 annual inspections plus seasonal drive-bys. Consider inspection software (HappyCo, zInspector, Inspectify) for mobile forms and photo management.
