1. Purpose & Legal Foundation

Tenant screening is simultaneously your most important quality-control function and your highest legal-risk activity. A consistent, documented screening process achieves two goals: (1) place high-quality tenants who pay rent and take care of the property, and (2) protect your company from Fair Housing discrimination claims.

Critical Legal Requirement: You must apply the SAME screening criteria to EVERY applicant, without exception. The moment you make an exception for one applicant and not another, you've created a potential Fair Housing violation. Document your criteria, apply them uniformly, and keep records of every decision.

This SOP is designed around three federal laws that govern tenant screening:

  • Fair Housing Act (FHA) — Prohibits discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin, disability, and familial status. Many states and cities add additional protected classes.
  • Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) — Requires adverse action notices when you deny an applicant based on information from a consumer report (credit report, background check).
  • Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA) — Prohibits discrimination in credit-related decisions, which includes rental applications.

2. Fair Housing Compliance Checklist

Review this checklist quarterly and before training any new staff member on screening:

Protected Classes (Federal)

  • Race
  • Color
  • Religion
  • Sex (includes sexual orientation and gender identity per 2021 HUD guidance)
  • National origin
  • Disability (physical and mental)
  • Familial status (families with children under 18, pregnant women)

Common State/Local Protected Classes (check your jurisdiction)

  • Source of income (Section 8 / Housing Choice Vouchers) — required in many states
  • Sexual orientation (if not covered by federal sex protections in your jurisdiction)
  • Gender identity
  • Marital status
  • Age
  • Military/veteran status
  • Immigration status
  • Criminal history (some jurisdictions restrict or ban criminal background screening)
Action Item: Look up your state and city's specific protected classes. Add them to this list. Train every team member. Ignorance is not a defense — and your employees' mistakes are your company's liability.

4. Step-by-Step Application Review Process

Step 1: Application Received

  • Verify application is complete — all fields filled, all required documents attached
  • Collect application fee (if applicable — check local regulations on fee amounts and refundability)
  • Log application in PM software with timestamp
  • Send acknowledgment email: "We received your application and will process it within [2-3] business days"
  • If incomplete, send specific list of missing items within 24 hours

Step 2: Run Screening Reports

  • Submit to screening vendor: credit report, criminal background, eviction history
  • FCRA requirement: applicant must have signed authorization form BEFORE you pull reports
  • Log report receipt date and reference number
  • Review reports within 24 hours of receipt

Step 3: Verify Income

  • Review submitted pay stubs — calculate gross monthly income
  • Call employer to verify: employment status, start date, salary/wage, likelihood of continued employment
  • Self-employed: review tax returns or bank statements (6 months minimum)
  • Calculate income-to-rent ratio: must meet 3x minimum
  • Document verification results

Step 4: Check Rental References

  • Contact current landlord (verify they are the actual landlord, not the applicant pretending)
  • Contact previous landlord (more reliable — current landlord may lie to get rid of a bad tenant)
  • Use the Reference Check Script (Section 5)
  • Document responses with date, time, and contact name

Step 5: Make Decision

  • Compare all results against your published screening criteria
  • Decision must be: Approve, Conditional Approve, or Deny
  • Document the specific criteria-based reason for the decision
  • If denying, prepare Adverse Action Notice (Section 7)
  • Notify applicant within 3 business days of decision

5. Reference Check Scripts

Previous Landlord Reference Check

Introduction: "Hi, this is [Name] from [Company]. I'm calling to verify a rental reference for [Applicant Name] who listed you as a previous landlord at [Address]. Do you have a few minutes?"

Questions to ask (document every answer):

  1. "Can you confirm [Applicant] rented from you at [Address]?"
  2. "What were the dates of their tenancy?"
  3. "What was the monthly rent amount?"
  4. "Did they pay rent on time consistently?"
  5. "Were there any lease violations during their tenancy?"
  6. "How did they leave the property? Was the security deposit returned in full?"
  7. "Did they give proper notice before moving out?"
  8. "Were there any noise complaints or neighbor issues?"
  9. "Would you rent to them again?" (The most important question)
Verification Tip: Before calling, independently verify the landlord's identity. Cross-reference the phone number with property records. Applicants have been known to list friends or family as "previous landlords." If the landlord's phone number matches the applicant's emergency contact, investigate further.

Employer Verification

Introduction: "Hi, this is [Name] from [Company]. I'm completing a rental application verification for [Applicant Name] who listed your company as their employer. Can you confirm their employment?"
  1. "Can you confirm [Applicant] is currently employed with [Company]?"
  2. "What is their job title?"
  3. "What is their start date?"
  4. "Can you confirm their annual salary/hourly wage is approximately $[amount stated on application]?"
  5. "Is this a full-time or part-time position?"

7. Adverse Action Notices (FCRA Compliance)

Legal Requirement: Under FCRA, you MUST provide a written adverse action notice to any applicant denied (or given less favorable terms) based on information from a consumer report. Failure to comply can result in statutory damages of $100-$1,000 per violation plus attorney's fees.

Required Elements of an Adverse Action Notice

  • Name, address, and phone number of the screening company that provided the report
  • Statement that the screening company did NOT make the adverse decision and cannot explain why
  • Notice of the applicant's right to obtain a free copy of the report within 60 days
  • Notice of the applicant's right to dispute the accuracy of the report with the screening company
  • The specific reason(s) for the adverse action (e.g., "insufficient income," "prior eviction," "credit score below minimum")

9. Documentation & Record Keeping

Keep all screening records for a minimum of 3 years (5 years recommended). This includes:

  • Completed application forms (including incomplete/withdrawn applications)
  • Signed screening authorization forms
  • Credit reports, criminal background reports, eviction reports
  • Income verification documentation
  • Reference check notes with dates and contact information
  • Decision documentation: approved, conditional, or denied with specific reasons
  • Adverse action notices (copies of all denial letters sent)
  • Any correspondence with applicants about their application
Why this matters: If a rejected applicant files a Fair Housing complaint, you'll need to produce your records showing consistent criteria applied to all applicants. If you can't produce records, you're assumed to be in violation. Documentation is your legal shield.

10. Edge Cases & Special Situations

Section 8 / Housing Choice Voucher Applicants

In many states and cities, you are legally required to accept Section 8 vouchers (source of income protection). Check your local laws. If required, screen voucher holders using the same criteria as all other applicants — the voucher portion of rent counts as verified income.

Co-Signers / Guarantors

When accepting a co-signer (common for first-time renters, students, or credit-rebuilding applicants):

  • The co-signer must meet ALL screening criteria independently
  • Co-signer income should be ≥ 5x monthly rent (they're covering their own obligations plus this lease)
  • Both the applicant and co-signer sign the lease
  • Co-signer agreement should be a separate, legally binding document

Applicants with No Credit History

Common with young adults, immigrants, and people who've always paid cash. Options:

  • Accept alternative credit references: utility bills, phone bills, insurance payments
  • Require additional security deposit (1.5x-2x)
  • Require a co-signer with established credit
  • Verify income at 4x rent instead of 3x

Emotional Support Animals (ESA) / Service Animals

You CANNOT deny an applicant or charge pet deposits/fees for legitimate service animals or ESAs. You CAN:

  • Request documentation of disability-related need (from a healthcare provider)
  • Deny if the specific animal poses a direct threat to safety or would cause substantial property damage
  • NOT charge pet rent, pet deposits, or pet fees for service animals/ESAs
